The D365 vs Salesforce debate is one of the most searched questions in enterprise software. Both are mature, capable platforms — yet choosing the wrong one for your business context will cost you years of painful workarounds.

This isn't a vendor-neutral comparison. We implement Dynamics 365. But we've also worked with clients who migrated *from* Salesforce to D365, and some who should have stayed on Salesforce. Our goal here is honest analysis.

## The Core Difference

Dynamics 365 is Microsoft's business application platform. It lives inside the Microsoft ecosystem — Azure, Office 365, Teams, Power BI, Power Automate. If your business runs on Microsoft, D365 is the natural extension.

Salesforce is a standalone CRM-first platform. Its strength is the depth of its sales and marketing automation tooling, its massive AppExchange marketplace, and its dominant position in sales-led organisations.

## When D365 Wins

### 1. You're Already on Microsoft 365

The native integration between D365 and Teams, Outlook, and SharePoint is genuinely seamless. Sales reps can log calls from Outlook, pull account data into Teams meetings, and generate proposals from Word templates — without switching tabs.

### 2. You Need ERP + CRM on One Platform

D365 is unique in offering Finance, Supply Chain, Field Service, and Commerce alongside its CRM modules. If you want a single platform for front-office and back-office operations, there's no equivalent.

### 3. Total Cost of Ownership

D365 licensing is typically 20–40% less than comparable Salesforce tiers, particularly for mid-market companies. Microsoft's per-user pricing and enterprise agreements frequently include D365 modules as add-ons to existing M365 contracts.

## When Salesforce Wins

### 1. You Have a Large, Complex Sales Motion

Salesforce's Sales Cloud is the gold standard for complex, multi-touch B2B sales processes. Its reporting flexibility, forecasting tools, and sales-specific UX are still ahead of D365 Sales in most benchmarks.

### 2. You Need the AppExchange

Over 4,000 pre-built integrations. If your stack includes tools with deep Salesforce connectors (Marketo, ZoomInfo, Outreach), switching to D365 means rebuilding those connections.

### 3. You Don't Need ERP Integration

If your back-office already runs on SAP or Oracle and isn't changing, D365's ERP advantage is irrelevant. You'd be paying for platform breadth you don't use.

## The Decision Framework

Ask yourself these four questions:

1. **Are we a Microsoft shop?** If yes, D365 has a structural advantage.
2. **Do we need ERP + CRM on one system?** If yes, D365 is the only serious option.
3. **Is our primary need sales automation?** If yes, evaluate Salesforce carefully.
4. **What's our 5-year roadmap?** A platform decision at this level should account for where you're going, not just where you are.

## Migration Costs

If you're considering switching from one to the other, factor in:

- Data migration (contacts, accounts, opportunities, attachments)
- Custom workflow recreation
- Integration rebuilds
- Retraining (typically 2–4 weeks for end users)

We've run migrations in both directions. They're never trivial, but they're manageable with the right scoping.

## Our Recommendation

For UK and UAE mid-market businesses with existing Microsoft infrastructure, Dynamics 365 is almost always the better commercial decision. For sales-led organisations with complex sales motions and no appetite for ERP consolidation, Salesforce remains competitive.
